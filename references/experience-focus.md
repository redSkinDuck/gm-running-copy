# Experience-First Focus

Do not decide prep focus from the game system alone. The system provides rules context, but the module's promised table experience and the user's GM goal decide what to emphasize.

Before choosing checks, identify:

- What experience the module promises players.
- What experience the GM wants to preserve or strengthen.
- Whether the module is investigation, combat adventure, emotional drama, horror, sandbox, performance, survival, mystery, travel, political play, or a hybrid.
- Which parts of the original text are load-bearing for that experience.
- Which parts can be loosened, personalized, cut, or rewritten.

Use the following as optional check families, not system-bound rules.

## Investigation Structure

Use when the module depends on discovery, deduction, mystery, testimony, records, or hidden truth.

Check:

- Clue chain
- Clue redundancy
- Failed-roll handling
- Information bottlenecks
- NPC testimony consistency
- Reveal timing
- Whether players can form useful hypotheses

## Combat Adventure

Use when the module depends on tactical challenge, expedition play, dungeon flow, survival pressure, or action set pieces.

Check:

- Encounter difficulty
- Resource pressure
- Rest logic
- Map readability
- Enemy goals and tactics
- Terrain and movement
- Reward placement
- Whether combat supports the intended story rhythm

## Emotional Drama

Use when the module depends on relationships, memory, identity, regret, romance, grief, obsession, trust, betrayal, or performance.

Check:

- PC identity pressure
- Player authorship
- Emotional trigger timing
- Relationship prompts
- Co-creation opportunities
- Read-aloud support
- Whether key scenes give players enough room to express themselves

## Horror and Suspense

Use when the module depends on dread, uncertainty, taboo, pursuit, body horror, cosmic horror, isolation, or mounting unease.

Check:

- Information control
- Atmosphere escalation
- Safety boundaries
- Reveal pacing
- False certainty
- Pressure without railroading
- Whether fear comes from playable situations, not only exposition

## Sandbox and Faction Play

Use when the module depends on open movement, factions, active NPC agendas, territory, clocks, or player-led direction.

Check:

- Location relationships
- Faction goals
- Timelines and clocks
- Consequences for delay
- Re-entry points after player detours
- NPC reactions
- Whether the GM can improvise without losing the module's spine

## Performance and Set-Piece Play

Use when the module depends on a climactic event, show, ritual, trial, debate, concert, heist, chase, or other highly staged sequence.

Check:

- Build-up
- Spotlight distribution
- Player contribution
- Stage directions
- Sensory description
- Failure states
- Whether the promised set piece actually happens at the table

## Rules Context

After identifying the experience focus, use the system only to translate those needs into mechanics:

- Rolls, DCs, saves, SAN checks, madness checks, clocks, resources, combat actions, downtime, or other procedures.
- System-specific terminology and constraints.
- Compatibility with character creation and advancement.

Avoid writing notes that imply "this system always means this prep style."
